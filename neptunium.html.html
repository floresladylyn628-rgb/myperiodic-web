Neptunium is a silvery, radioactive metal in the actinide series. It was the first synthetic transuranium element. It is formed either artificially in nuclear reactors or naturally in trace amounts in uranium ores, neptunium is highly unstable and primarily used in nuclear science, such as in the production of plutonium-238 for space missions.

Neptunium was first synthesized on June 8, 1940, by American physicists Edwin McMillan and Philip Abelson at the University of California, Berkeley. They bombarded uranium-238 with neutrons in the Berkeley cyclotron, creating the isotope neptunium-239. This discovery marked the first time a synthetic element had been produced beyond uranium in the periodic table, opening the door to the discovery of other transuranium elements.

Neptunium has various applications, including:


Nuclear research: Neptunium is used in research to study nuclear reactions and properties of actinides.


Neutron detection: Neptunium-237 is used in specialized neutron detectors due to its ability to capture neutrons.


Potential nuclear fuel: Some studies explore neptunium as a potential fuel in advanced nuclear reactors, though this is not yet widely implemented.
