Osmium is a bluish-white, dense, and brittle metal, recognized as the densest naturally occurring element with a density of about 22.59 g/cm3.

Osmium was discovered in 1803 by English chemist Smithson Tennant during his study of platinum ores. Tennant found that when platinum ore was dissolved in aqua regia, an insoluble residue remained. Upon further analysis, he identified two new elements: osmium and iridium. Osmium was named after the Greek word "osme," meaning "smell," due to the strong, pungent odor of its compound osmium tetroxide, which forms during chemical reactions.

Osmium has various applications, including:


Fountain pen tips: Used in alloys for durable pen nibs.


Instrument bearings: Incorporated into precision components like pivots in compasses and scientific instruments.


Microscopy: Osmium tetroxide is employed in electron microscopy to stain biological specimens, enhancing contrast by reacting with lipids.
