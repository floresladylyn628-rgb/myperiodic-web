Praseodymium is a soft, malleable, ductile, and has a bright silvery appearance, that tarnishes quickly in air due to oxidation. It typically makes up about 5% of the total rare earth content in these minerals.

Praseodymium was discovered in 1885 by Austrian chemist Carl Auer von Welsbach. While studying the rare earth element didymium (previously thought to be a single substance), Welsbach successfully separated it into distinct elemens: praseodymium and neodymium. The "praseodymium" comes from the Greek words "prasois" (green) and "didymos" (twin), reflecting the green color of its salts and its close association with neodymium.

Praseodymium has various applications, including:


Ceramics and glass: Praseodymium-doped glass filters are used in welding goggles and scientific instruments to block infrared radiation.


Nuclear: Praseodymium is used in alloys for control rods in nuclear reactors due to its ability to absorb neutrons.


Optical applications: Praseodymium is used in optical fibers and lasers for its ability to amplify light signals.
