Neodymium is a silvery-white, soft, and malleable metal, belonging to the lanthanide series of rare earth elements. It is moderately reactive, tarnishing in air and reacting with water to form hydroxides.

Neodymium was discovered in 1885 by Austrian chemist Carl Auer von Welsbach, who separated it from a substance called didymium, which was previously thought to be a single element. Using electroscopic analysis, Welsbach demonstrated that didymium was actually a mixture of two distinct elements: neodymium and praseodymium. The name neodymium is derived from the Greek words "neos" (new) and "didymos" (twin).

Neodymium has various applications, including:


Glass coloring: Neodymium is used to color glass purple, pink, or red, producing vibrant shades for decorative purposes.


Ceramics: Neodymium is used in ceramic capacitors and other electronic components due to its insulating properties.


Renewable energy: Neodymium magnets are vital for wind turbines and electric vehicles, playing a key role in the transition to clean energy technologies.
