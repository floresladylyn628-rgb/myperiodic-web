Molybdenum is a chemical element, symbolized as Mo, and is a transition metal. It's a silver-white metal with a high melting point and excellent strength at high temperatures. Molybdenum is used in various applications, including steel production (as an alloying element), high-temperature applications, catalysts, and industrial equipment. Its unique properties make it useful for high-performance applications, particularly in extreme environments.

Molybdenum was discovered by Carl Wilhelm Scheele in 1778, and first isolated by Peter Jacob Hjelm in 1783. The name "molybdenum" comes from the Greek word "molybdos," meaning "lead," as it was often confused with lead ores. Initially, molybdenum was difficult to extract and purify, but with advancements in technology, it became widely used in various applications, including steel production and high-temperature applications. Today, molybdenum is an essential element in many industrial processes.

Molybdenum has various applications, including:


Steel production: Molybdenum is used as an alloying element to enhance strength, hardness, and resistance to corrosion and high temperatures.


Industrial equipment: Molybdenum is used in wear-resistant parts, such as piston rings and engine components.


Catalysts: Molybdenum is used as a catalyst in the petroleum industry for hydrodesulfurization and other processes.
