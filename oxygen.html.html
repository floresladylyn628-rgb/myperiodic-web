Oxygen is a colorless, odorless, and tasteless gas essential for life, making up about 21% of Earth's atmosphere. It is a chemical element with the symbol O and atomic number 8. Oxygen is highly reactive, readily forming compounds with almost all other elements. It is crucial for respiration in most living organisms and for combustion.

Antoine Lavoisier is credited with both discovering and naming oxygen. While Joseph Priestly and Carl Wilhelm Scheele had previously isolated oxygen, Lavoisier's work in the late 1770s was crucial in understanding its role in combustion and respiration and establishing it as a chemical element. He also coined the name "oxygen" form the Greek roots meaning "acid-forming".

Oxygen has various applications, including:


Glass and ceramics: Oxygen enhances combustion for melting and shaping glass and ceramics.


Pulp and paper: Oxygen is used in bleaching cellulose and removing lignin in paper production


Life support: Essential for oxygenating patients in emergency situations, during surgery, and in other critical care scenarios.
