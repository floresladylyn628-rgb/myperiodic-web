Nickel is a silvery-white, ductile, and corrosion-resistant metal with the atomic number 28 and symbol Ni. It's known for its high melting point, strength, and ability to withstand extreme temperatures, making it useful in applications like stainless steel, coins, and industrial processes.

Nickel was discovered by Swedish chemist Axel Fredrik Cronstedt in 1751. The name "nickel" comes from the German word "Kupfernickel," meaning "devil's copper," due to the ore's resemblance to copper and the troubles it caused miners. Nickel has since become a crucial element in various industrial applications, including stainless steel, alloys, and coins.

Nickel has various applications, including:


Coins: Nickel is used in coins, such as the US nickel, due to its corrosion resistance and durability.


Aerospace: Nickel-based alloys are used in aerospace applications, including jet engines and gas turbines.


Stainless steel: Nickel is a key component in stainless steel, providing corrosion resistance and strength.
