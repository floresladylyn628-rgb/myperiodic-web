Polonium is highly radioactive, silvery-gray metal. It is notable for its intense radioactvity, producing heat through alpha particle emission.

Polonium was discovered in 1898 by Marie Curie and her husband, Pierre Curie, while they were studying the radioactive properties of uranium ores such as pitchblende. It was the first element they discovered during their groundbreaking research into radioactvity. Marie named the element polonium after her homeland, Poland (Polonia), as a tribute to her native country, which at the time was under partition and lacked independence.

Polonium has various applications, including:


Nuclear science: Used as a neutron initiator in nuclear weapons to trigger chain reactions.


Heat source: Its alpha radiation generates heat, powering thermoelectric generators in spacecraft.


Poisoning: Infamously used in assassinations due to its high toxicity and radioactvity.
