Promethium is a radioactive rare earth element, belonging to the lanthanide series. It is extremely rare in nature. It is found in uranium and thorium ores as a result of radiactive decay.

It was first predicted by Dmitri Mendeleev, who suspected a gap in the periodic table. Many attempts to discover the element but failed. It wasn't until 1945 that American chemists Jacob Marinsky, Lawrence Glendenin, and Charles Coryell successfully isolated promethium from uranium fission products at Oak Ridge National Laboratory. The element was named after Prometheus, the titan in Greek mythology who stole fire from the gods, reflecting its radioactive and energetic nature.

Promethium has various applications, including:


Atomic batteries: Used in long-lasting batteries for satellites and pacemakers.


Luminous paints: Glows in the dark for watch dials and safety signs.


Space exploartions: Powers spacecrafts instruments.
